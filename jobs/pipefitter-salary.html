What Does a Pipefitter Do?

Pipefitters assemble, install, and maintain pipe systems for steam, water, heating, cooling, and industrial processes.

How to Become a Pipefitter

Education: High school diploma + apprenticeship (4-5 years)

Certifications: State pipefitting license; welding certs

1. Earn a high school diploma.

2. Complete a pipefitting apprenticeship (4-5 years).

3. Learn welding and piping codes.

4. Obtain required licenses.

5. Specialize.

Career path: Apprentice → Journeyman → Foreman → General Foreman → Piping Superintendent

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a pipefitter make?
▼
The national median salary is $63,560 per year.
Is pipefitting a good career?
▼
Yes. Strong union wages and benefits.
How long does it take?
▼
4-5 years through an apprenticeship.
What is the difference between a plumber and pipefitter?
▼
Plumbers work on building water/drainage. Pipefitters work with industrial high-pressure systems.
Do pipefitters need to weld?
▼
Yes. Welding is a critical skill.
Methodology and data sources

Salary data is based on the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OES) program. National median, 10th percentile, and 90th percentile figures are sourced from the most recent BLS OES release. State-level salary estimates are calculated by applying regional price parity adjustments from the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) to the national median. Actual state-specific BLS data may differ. Job growth projections are from the BLS Employment Projections program. Education and certification requirements are based on BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook descriptions. All figures are approximate and updated periodically.
