What Does a HVAC Technician Do?

HVAC technicians install, maintain, and repair heating, ventilation, air conditioning, and refrigeration systems.

How to Become a HVAC Technician

Education: Postsecondary certificate or apprenticeship

Certifications: EPA Section 608 required; NATE preferred

1. Complete HVAC training (6 months to 5 years).

2. Obtain EPA Section 608 certification.

3. Gain field experience.

4. Earn NATE certification.

5. Consider specializing.

Career path:Apprentice → HVAC Tech → Senior Tech → Supervisor → Contractor

AI & HVAC Technician: What You Need to Know in 2026

How AI Is Changing This Role

AI is enhancing hvac technician work through smart estimating software, predictive maintenance systems, and IoT-connected building management. While the hands-on craft skills remain essential and difficult to automate, AI tools are making experienced hvac technicians more efficient and enabling better project outcomes.

How Top Performers Use AI

  • Using AI-powered estimating and bidding software to produce more accurate project quotes in a fraction of the time
  • Leveraging predictive maintenance platforms and IoT sensors to identify potential issues before they become costly failures
  • Employing drone inspection tools and AI-assisted diagnostics to assess conditions in hard-to-reach or dangerous areas

AI Skills That Help You Get Hired

Candidates who combine traditional trade expertise with digital literacy stand out. Familiarity with smart building systems, IoT integration, Building Information Modeling (BIM), and computer-aided design tools signals adaptability. Understanding how to interpret AI-generated maintenance recommendations is increasingly valued.

AI Salary Impact

HVAC Technicians who adopt AI estimating and smart building technologies earn 10-15% more through higher project efficiency and premium service offerings.

Methodology and data sources

Salary data is based on the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OES) program. National median, 10th percentile, and 90th percentile figures are sourced from the most recent BLS OES release. State-level salary estimates are calculated by applying regional price parity adjustments from the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) to the national median. Job growth projections are from the BLS Employment Projections program. Education and certification requirements are based on BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook descriptions. All figures are approximate and updated periodically.
