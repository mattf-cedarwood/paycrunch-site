Understanding Your Take-Home Pay

Your take-home pay (net pay) is what you actually receive after all deductions. The main deductions are federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and any pre-tax contributions like 401(k).

Federal Tax Brackets (2024)

The US uses a progressive tax system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. For a single filer: the first $11,600 is taxed at 10%, $11,601-$47,150 at 12%, $47,151-$100,525 at 22%, and so on up to 37% for income above $609,350.

No-Income-Tax States

Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Set the state tax rate to 0% if you live in one of these states.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this calculator?
This provides a reasonable estimate using 2024 federal tax brackets and standard deductions. Your actual pay depends on specific deductions, credits, W-4 settings, and local taxes. Consult a tax professional for precise numbers.
What is FICA tax?
FICA = Social Security (6.2% up to $168,600) + Medicare (1.45% on all earnings). Total: 7.65% for most workers. Your employer pays a matching 7.65%.
Should I contribute to a 401(k)?
Generally yes, especially if your employer matches. The match is free money. Even without a match, 401(k) contributions reduce your taxable income now and grow tax-deferred.
What take-home pay can I expect on $60K?
Roughly $45,000-$48,000/year take-home on a $60K salary (single, no 401k, average state tax). That's about $3,500-$3,700/month after all deductions.
