Understanding Your Paycheck

Every paycheck has mandatory deductions that reduce your gross pay to net pay. Understanding these deductions helps you budget accurately and plan your finances.

Federal Income Tax

Federal tax uses progressive brackets. Your employer withholds based on your W-4 form, filing status, and pay frequency. The 2024 standard deduction is $14,600 (Single) or $29,200 (Married Filing Jointly).

FICA Taxes

Social Security tax is 6.2% on earnings up to $168,600 (2024 cap). Medicare is 1.45% on all earnings, with an additional 0.9% on earnings over $200,000. These fund retirement and healthcare benefits.

State Income Tax

State taxes vary from 0% (9 states) to over 13% (California top bracket). Some states use flat rates while others have progressive brackets similar to federal.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much will my paycheck be after taxes?
Most workers take home 70-80% of gross pay after federal tax, state tax, Social Security (6.2%), and Medicare (1.45%). On a $1,000 gross paycheck, expect roughly $750-800 net depending on your tax bracket, filing status, and state.
What is FICA tax and why is it deducted?
FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It funds Social Security (6.2% up to $168,600 in 2024) and Medicare (1.45% on all earnings, plus 0.9% on earnings over $200,000). Your employer pays a matching amount. Total FICA for most workers is 7.65%.
Which states have no income tax?
Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. New Hampshire and Tennessee tax investment income only. Living in these states means a larger paycheck.
How does filing status affect my paycheck?
Filing status determines your standard deduction and tax bracket thresholds. Married Filing Jointly gets a $29,200 standard deduction (2024) vs $14,600 for Single. This means less taxable income and lower tax withholding per paycheck.
What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?
Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is what you actually receive after federal tax, state tax, FICA, and any voluntary deductions like 401(k) or health insurance are subtracted.
