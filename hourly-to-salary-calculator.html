How to Convert Hourly Wage to Annual Salary

The formula is simple: Hourly Rate x Hours Per Week x Weeks Per Year = Annual Salary. For a standard full-time schedule (40 hours/week, 52 weeks/year), multiply your hourly rate by 2,080 to get your annual gross salary.

For example, $20/hour x 2,080 hours = $41,600 per year before taxes. If you take two weeks of unpaid vacation, use 2,000 hours instead, giving you $40,000.

Why This Matters for Job Seekers

When comparing job offers, you need apples-to-apples numbers. A position offering $22/hour sounds different from one advertising $42,000/year, but the hourly job actually pays more ($22 x 2,080 = $45,760). Always convert to the same unit before comparing.

Understanding Take-Home Pay

These figures are gross (pre-tax) earnings. Your actual paycheck will be reduced by federal income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and state income tax. Most full-time workers take home roughly 70-80% of their gross pay. States like Texas, Florida, and Nevada have no state income tax, giving residents a higher take-home percentage.

Part-Time and Overtime

For part-time workers, enter your actual weekly hours. For overtime, remember that federal law requires time-and-a-half (1.5x) for hours beyond 40 per week. Calculate base pay at 40 hours, then add overtime hours separately at 1.5x your rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is $15 an hour annually?
$15/hour x 40 hours x 52 weeks = $31,200 per year. That's $2,600/month, $600/week, or $120/day before taxes.
How much is $20 an hour annually?
$20/hour x 40 hours x 52 weeks = $41,600 per year. Monthly: ~$3,467. Weekly: $800. Daily: $160.
How much is $25 an hour annually?
$25/hour x 40 hours x 52 weeks = $52,000 per year. Monthly: ~$4,333. Weekly: $1,000. Daily: $200.
How many work hours in a year?
A standard full-time year has 2,080 hours (40 hrs/wk x 52 weeks). With 2 weeks vacation: 2,000 hours. With 4 weeks: 1,920 hours.
Does this include taxes?
No. This shows gross (pre-tax) earnings. Expect to take home 70-80% after federal tax, state tax, Social Security, and Medicare deductions.
Salary vs hourly — which is better?
Hourly gives overtime protection. Salary often includes benefits like insurance and PTO. Compare total compensation, not just base pay.
